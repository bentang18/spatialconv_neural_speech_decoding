# Fang et al. 2026 - RPNT: Robust Pre-trained Neural Transformer for Generalized Motor Decoding

## Citation
Fang, H., Canfield, R.A., Ouchi, T., Macagno, B., Shlizerman, E., & Orsborn, A.L. (2026). RPNT: Robust Pre-trained Neural Transformer - A Pathway for Generalized Motor Decoding. *Preprint*. arXiv:2601.17641v1.

## Setup
- **Recording modalities**: Two distinct recording types used in parallel:
  - **Public microelectrode benchmark** (Perich et al. 2018): Utah arrays recording multi-unit spiking activity (spike counts), binned at 20 ms windows (50 units), 1 s trials
  - **Neuropixel dataset** (Canfield et al. 2025): High-density Neuropixel 1.0 probes; 17 recording sites (S1-S17) distributed across PMd and M1 regions, single probe inserted per session
- **Species**: Non-human primates (NHPs) -- rhesus macaques
- **Datasets**:
  - **Public benchmark**: 4 subjects (monkeys C, J, M, T); 111 sessions, 43 recording hours; M1 and PMd regions
  - **Neuropixel dataset**: 1 subject (monkey B) implanted with multi-modal chamber over frontal motor cortex; 17 recordings across approximately one year
- **Tasks**: Center-Out (CO) reaching task (discrete 8-direction targets) and Random Target (RT) reaching task (continuous random target acquisition); both produce hand velocity (X and Y) as the decoding target
- **Decoding target**: Hand velocity (X, Y coordinates), evaluated with R^2

## Problem Statement
Existing pretrained neural transformer models (NDT, NDT2, NDT3, POYO, POYO+, PoSSM) can only partially address variation in neural recordings. Three specific gaps remain:

1. **Cross-site generalization**: Models fail to generalize to unseen brain sites or different recording configurations (Jude et al. 2022; Karpowicz et al. 2024; Le et al. 2025). Current positional encodings are designed for sequential data and do not encode physical brain coordinates, making zero-shot generalization to new spatial configurations impossible.

2. **Non-stationarity**: Neural signals drift over time (Chestek et al. 2011). Standard self-attention assumes stationary input distributions. No existing pretrained neural model explicitly handles the local temporal non-stationarity of population spiking activity.

3. **Representation quality**: Existing SSL objectives (masked autoencoding / denoising) train the model to interpolate missing values, producing representations suited for offline denoising but not for learning causal neural dynamics -- the underlying generative process that gives rise to behavior.

## Method

### Architecture Overview
RPNT is a transformer encoder with three novel components (MRoPE, context-based attention, causal masking + contrastive SSL) layered onto a standard multi-head attention backbone. The full model decouples a **temporal encoder** (processes one site at a time, applies MRoPE + context-based attention) from an optional **spatial encoder** (MHA across sites at each timestep, captures cross-site functional connectivity).

### 1. Multidimensional Rotary Positional Embedding (MRoPE)
Standard RoPE encodes only temporal position. MRoPE extends RoPE to M dimensions to jointly encode all recording configuration metadata:

**For the Neuropixel dataset (M = 3D):** encodes spatial x-coordinate, y-coordinate, and time t. The model dimension d_model is partitioned into three equal groups. Separate rotation frequencies are used per dimension:
- Spatial x: theta_i^(x) = 1 / 5000^(2i/d_coord)  (lower frequency f = 5000 for coherence across nearby sites)
- Spatial y: theta_i^(y) = 1 / 5000^(2i/d_coord)
- Temporal: theta_i^(t) = 1 / 10000^(2i/d_coord)  (standard RoPE frequency f = 10000)

The complete 3D rotation matrix R_3D(x_s, y_s, t) is block-diagonal: diag(R_x(x), R_y(y), R_t(t)). MRoPE is applied to query and key vectors during attention computation. The attention score between tokens at positions i and j becomes a function of relative distances (x_i - x_j, y_i - y_j, t_i - t_j), preserving the RoPE relative-position property. This yields zero-shot generalization: any new recording site with continuous (x, y) coordinates can be immediately embedded without retraining.

**For the public benchmark (M = 4D):** encodes behavior type (CO/RT), subject identity (C/J/M/T), recording time (day/month/year), and temporal bin t. Discrete string-valued metadata are mapped to scalar indices (e.g., {CO, RT} -> {0, 1}; {c, j, m, t} -> {0, 1, 2, 3}; recording date normalized alphabetically). Frequencies are set to {10, 100, 1000, 10000} for the four dimensions respectively.

**Ablation result**: MRoPE achieves R^2 of 0.8515 (T-RT) and 0.6612 (B-CS), versus standard RoPE at 0.8226 / 0.6484 and sinusoidal PE at 0.8260 / 0.6242 -- approximately 3% improvement over standard RoPE on the cross-task benchmark.

### 2. Context-Based Attention Mechanism
To handle non-stationarity (neural signals drift over time), RPNT replaces standard static self-attention with a context-adaptive convolution-augmented mechanism:

**Context generation**: Given current input X and historical data H_hist (the K most recent time bins, receding window), a context vector c is computed via attention pooling: c = AttentionPool(MLP(H_hist)) in R^(B x D_context).

**Dynamic kernel generation**: The context vector is mapped to per-head 2D convolution kernels: K_h = reshape(softmax(MLP_kernel(c)), [K1, K2]), where (K1, K2) are kernel dimensions (default 9x9, swept in ablation). Each attention head learns a specialized kernel.

**Kernel-enhanced attention score**: The standard attention matrix QK^T / sqrt(d) is convolved with K_h, then combined with causal masking: A_kernel = Conv2D(QK^T / sqrt(d), K_h) * M_causal, where M_causal[i, j] = 1 iff j <= i.

**Ablation result**: Replacing context-based attention with standard self-attention causes a ~5% performance drop (R^2: 0.8515 -> 0.8024 on T-RT; 0.6612 -> 0.5024 on B-CS).

### 3. Neural Transformer Encoder Architecture
The temporal encoder processes input X in R^(B x S x T x N) (batch, sites, time, neurons) with corresponding metadata:
- Initial projection: H_s^(0) = Linear(X_{:,s,:,:})
- L transformer layers: H_s^(l+1) = H_s^(l) + ContextAttn(LN(H_s^(l)), MRoPE(configurations))

The optional spatial encoder appends standard MHA across sites at each timestep (equation 7), enabling cross-site functional connectivity modeling while naturally preserving causality.

### 4. Causal Masked Neural Modeling (SSL Pretraining)
RPNT departs from standard MAE-style denoising (bidirectional reconstruction) in two key ways:

**Causal constraint**: At masked position (t, n), reconstruction uses only unmasked inputs from t' <= t. This transforms MAE's interpolation objective into a causal extrapolation task, aligned with the autoregressive nature of neural population dynamics.

**Uniform random masking**: Masking ratios for both neurons and time are drawn independently per batch from Uniform(0, 1), i.e., p_m ~ U(0, 1). This eliminates the mask-ratio hyperparameter and ensures diverse reconstruction difficulty during pretraining. Fixed ratios (0.25, 0.5, 0.75 in any combination) consistently underperform uniform random masking.

**Poisson reconstruction loss**: Since spike counts follow a Poisson distribution, RPNT uses Poisson NLL rather than MSE: L_recon = sum over masked (i,t,n) of [lambda_{:,t,n} - x_{:,t,n} * log(lambda_{:,t,n} + eps)], where lambda = Decoder(Z_{:,t,d}).

**Cross-site contrastive loss**: An auxiliary objective encourages site-invariant representations. For each site s_i at time t, positive pairs are same-site representations averaged across time; negative pairs are different-site representations. Loss is InfoNCE with temperature tau = 0.1: L_contrast = -1/S * sum_i log[exp(sim(z_i, z_i) / tau) / sum_j exp(sim(z_i, z_j) / tau)]. Combined objective: L = L_recon + mu * L_contrast.

**Ablation result**: Adding contrastive loss improves B-CS from 0.6267 to 0.6612 (Table 3 in appendix).

### Cross-Site / Cross-Subject Mechanism
The combination of MRoPE with physical coordinates and the contrastive pretraining objective drives cross-site generalization. MRoPE ensures that the model's notion of "position" is grounded in continuous brain space, so new recording sites with coordinates not seen during pretraining can still be embedded via the rotational structure (zero-shot generalization property proven in Appendix F). The cross-site contrastive loss further aligns representations from different sites, making the encoder site-invariant at the representation level while still being site-specific at the attention score level (via the coordinate-dependent rotations).

For the public benchmark (cross-subject), MRoPE encodes subject identity as one of the 4D dimensions, allowing the model to learn subject-conditioned representations during pretraining.

### Downstream Evaluation (Supervised Fine-Tuning, SFT)
A lightweight MLP task head is attached to the last layer of the temporal encoder: y_hat_t = MLP_task(H_{:,t}). SFT optimizes MSE on hand velocity. Two regimes:
- **Full-SFT**: fine-tunes on all available training sessions of the downstream task
- **FS-SFT (few-shot SFT)**: fine-tunes on a single downstream session only

## Cross-Patient Relevance
**Applicability to cross-patient speech decoding from intra-op uECOG (~20 patients, ~15 min each)**

1. **MRoPE is directly the most relevant contribution**: uECOG electrode grids have known physical (x, y) coordinates on the cortical surface, and different patients will have grids placed at different locations and orientations. MRoPE's 3D-RoPE (or extended 4D-RoPE with patient identity) directly addresses this: each electrode's spatial coordinate is encoded as a continuous physical position, enabling zero-shot generalization to new patients whose electrode coordinates were never seen during pretraining. This is the single most directly transferable mechanism in the paper.

2. **Cross-patient pretraining via SSL is validated here**: This paper demonstrates that SSL pretraining on multi-session, multi-subject spike data improves downstream decoding with only one fine-tuning session (FS-SFT). The same paradigm applies to uECOG: pretrain on all 20 patients' unlabeled neural activity, then fine-tune each patient's session with the 15-minute labeled window.

3. **Context-based attention addresses intra-op non-stationarity**: uECOG signals during intra-op procedures are particularly non-stationary (anesthesia fluctuations, patient arousal changes, task engagement). The context-based convolution kernel mechanism adapts the attention pattern based on local history, which would be directly beneficial.

4. **The 4D-MRoPE patient-ID dimension is useful but limited**: Encoding patient identity as a discrete index (as RPNT does for subjects in the public benchmark) works when patient identities are known at training time. For intra-op uECOG, new patients are encountered at test time. The continuous spatial coordinate approach (as in the Neuropixel 3D-RoPE) is more appropriate because it generalizes to new (x, y) locations by virtue of the rotation structure, without requiring the patient ID to be in the training set.

5. **Data scale is more favorable than BIT**: Unlike BIT, which required hundreds of hours of Utah array data, RPNT achieves strong cross-site generalization with 16 Neuropixel sessions. 20 intra-op uECOG patients (even at 15 min each) would give ~5 hours -- sparse, but the FS-SFT regime with 10% training ratio (Figure 4) suggests pretrained RPNT can be useful with very limited downstream data.

6. **Causal masking SSL is beneficial for online BCI**: RPNT's causal SSL objective (reconstruct only from past context) produces representations suited for online/causal decoding, unlike MAE-style bidirectional approaches. Intra-op speech decoding, if deployed online, requires causal inference.

7. **Key adaptation required**: RPNT was developed for motor cortex spike count data. For uECOG high-gamma power (or broadband features), the Poisson reconstruction loss would need to be replaced with Gaussian/MSE loss. The architecture itself (temporal encoder with MRoPE) is modality-agnostic beyond the loss function.

## Limitations

1. **Motor cortex only**: RPNT was developed and validated exclusively on M1 and PMd recordings during reaching tasks. Speech decoding involves sensorimotor and language areas (IFG, STG, SMC) with different functional organization. The pretraining data has no speech-related neural content.

2. **NHP only**: All data comes from non-human primates. Human speech decoding involves linguistic processing not present in reaching tasks. MRoPE's coordinate structure would still apply, but the pretrained representations may not carry useful priors for speech.

3. **No spike data in uECOG context**: RPNT uses spike count inputs. uECOG records local field potentials (LFPs), typically processed as high-gamma power envelopes (70-150 Hz). The input tokenization, preprocessing pipeline, and Poisson loss assumption would need to be redesigned for LFP-derived features.

4. **Simple tasks**: Center-Out and Random Target reaching are highly stereotyped. Speech production involves much richer behavioral variability. The pretraining diversity is limited to two task types across 4 subjects.

5. **Basal ganglia and frontal cortex excluded**: The authors acknowledge this -- "modeling was limited to cortical motor areas, excluding brain regions such as the frontal cortex and the basal ganglia, which play a key role in motor control." Speech-relevant areas like Broca's area (IFG) are frontal cortex regions not covered.

6. **Cross-modality transfer is limited**: Transfer from neuropixel to public benchmark is poor (Table 9 in appendix: B-CS 0.6537 from public pretraining vs. 0.6612 from within-neuropixel pretraining). This suggests the model is sensitive to recording modality and that pretraining on mismatched data provides limited benefit. Pretraining on spike data from motor cortex and then fine-tuning on uECOG speech data would likely show a large domain gap.

7. **Single modality**: RPNT is explicitly single-modality (neural spikes). The authors note that multimodal RPNT (e.g., incorporating video, audio, or behavioral signals) is future work.

8. **Subject identity as discrete index limits generalization**: For the public benchmark, subject identity is encoded as a discrete integer index. New subjects unseen during pretraining cannot be properly embedded unless the spatial coordinate approach (as in Neuropixel) is used instead.

## Reusable Ideas

1. **MRoPE with physical electrode coordinates (the key idea)**: Encode each electrode's (x, y) cortical surface position as the spatial dimensions of MRoPE, using lower base frequency (f = 5000) for spatial dimensions than temporal dimensions (f = 10000). This gives the attention mechanism a physically grounded spatial prior: nearby electrodes attend to each other more strongly, and the rotational structure enables zero-shot generalization to new electrode positions not seen during pretraining. For uECOG grids, (x, y) can be the grid column/row index (in mm) derived from the known inter-electrode spacing. Directly applicable.

2. **4D-MRoPE for metadata-conditioned pretraining**: Beyond spatial coordinates and time, encode session-level metadata (recording date, subject ID, task type) as additional RoPE dimensions. This is a general recipe for any multi-session, multi-subject neural dataset: assign a unique dimension to each metadata axis and partition the model's embedding dimension accordingly. For uECOG cross-patient pretraining, dimensions could be: electrode x, electrode y, patient age/hemisphere/grid type (continuous proxy), and time.

3. **Context-based convolution-augmented attention for non-stationarity**: Generate per-head 2D convolution kernels dynamically from a pooled history context vector. The kernel modulates the attention score matrix after QK^T computation. This adds minimal parameters (the MLP_kernel module) while substantially improving robustness to signal drift. Directly applicable to any domain with non-stationary signals.

4. **Causal masking as SSL pretraining objective for online-capable representations**: Instead of MAE's bidirectional reconstruction, mask and reconstruct causally (only use past context). This produces representations that are implicitly trained for online extrapolation rather than offline interpolation. For BCI applications requiring real-time decoding, causal SSL should be the default choice over MAE-style objectives.

5. **Uniform random masking ratio (U(0,1) per batch)**: Eliminating the mask ratio as a hyperparameter by sampling it from Uniform(0,1) at each batch. This simple stochastic strategy consistently outperforms fixed ratios and removes the need for mask-ratio tuning, which is otherwise dataset-dependent. Directly applicable to any SSL pretraining on neural data.

6. **Poisson reconstruction loss for spike count data / adaptable loss for other modalities**: The Poisson NLL (instead of MSE) is appropriate for spike count inputs and benefits from the non-negative, integer-valued nature of spikes. For uECOG high-gamma power features (continuous, non-negative), a Gamma or log-normal NLL would be the analogous principled choice. The principle of matching the reconstruction loss to the signal's generative distribution is reusable.

7. **Cross-site contrastive loss for site-invariant representations**: InfoNCE loss where same-site representations across time are positive pairs and different-site representations are negative pairs. Temperature tau = 0.1. This auxiliary objective encourages the encoder to learn site-invariant features useful for cross-site transfer. Analogously, a cross-patient contrastive loss (same-patient recordings are positive pairs, different-patient recordings are negative pairs) could drive patient-invariant representation learning for cross-patient uECOG.

8. **Separate temporal and spatial encoders**: Decouple temporal dynamics modeling (within-site, across time, with MRoPE + context attention) from spatial interaction modeling (cross-site, within timestep, with standard MHA). This modularity allows the spatial encoder to be optionally added or removed, and its attention weights directly visualize functional connectivity. For cross-patient uECOG, the spatial encoder's cross-electrode attention weights could reveal which electrode pairs are functionally linked during speech production.

9. **Few-shot SFT (FS-SFT) regime**: Pretraining + fine-tuning on a single downstream session outperforms all baselines that were trained from scratch using all available sessions. This demonstrates that pretraining quality, not downstream data quantity, is the binding constraint. For 15-minute intra-op sessions, FS-SFT (pretrain on pooled patients, fine-tune on single patient) is precisely the correct training paradigm to adopt.

10. **Random neuron resampling for variable channel counts**: When a session has fewer neurons/electrodes than the batch-required maximum N, randomly resample (with replacement) the available neural activities to match shape (T, N). This avoids the need for zero-padding or separate handling of variable-width inputs, and because uniform masking is applied during pretraining, the replicated channels are treated stochastically rather than always seen together. Directly applicable to variable-count uECOG grids across patients.
