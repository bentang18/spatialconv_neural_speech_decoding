## Setup

- **Recording modality**: Intracortical multielectrode arrays (Utah arrays and probes), 30 kHz extracellular potentials band-pass filtered and thresholded into spike counts. Neural activity tokenized as binned spike counts (20 ms bins, 32-channel spatial patches).
- **Species / patient count**:
  - Pretraining: 30+ monkeys and humans (exact breakdown not given; pretraining spans diverse subjects from multiple labs)
  - Evaluation: 4 human participants (held out entirely from pretraining) and 4 monkey datasets (also held out from pretraining)
- **Brain region**: Primarily primary motor cortex (M1) and premotor cortex. Two additional evaluation datasets cover somatosensory cortex (S1) and oculomotor areas (FEF/MT).
- **Tasks during pretraining**: Reaching and grasping tasks from experimental paradigms; short repeated trials, motor covariate signals from limb kinematics (optical tracking), EMG, or force commands. Humans perform imagined/cued movements; behavioral signals are programmatically generated. Also includes closed-loop iBCI control trials.
- **Evaluation tasks (8 datasets)**: 2D + Click Cursor (human), 7D Reach/Grasp Kinematics FALCON H1 (human), 1D Grasp Force (human), 4D Bimanual cursor (human), 2D Finger Pose FALCON M2 (monkey), 16D Reach/Grasp EMG FALCON M1 (monkey), 1D Critical Stability Task (monkey), 2D Self-paced Reach (monkey).
- **Pretraining scale**: ~2000 hours (1750 hours after filtering; rounded to 2 khr in text), yielding ~1 billion neural tokens across ~3 million sequences.

---

## Problem Statement

Most neural decoding models are trained per-subject and per-task, requiring substantial calibration data for each new user or experimental setting. Cross-subject and cross-task generalization is limited because: (1) different neurons are recorded in each subject, so there is no direct neuron correspondence across subjects ("sensor variability"); (2) different tasks require different output dimensions; and (3) individual neural datasets are narrow in behavioral scope. Foundation models in NLP and computer vision have overcome analogous heterogeneity by scaling training data and model capacity jointly. This paper tests whether the same approach can work for intracortical spiking data, aiming to produce a generalist decoder that benefits downstream users, especially those with limited calibration data (< 1.5 hours), where the cost of data collection is a barrier.

## Method

### Architecture

NDT3 is a **causal autoregressive Transformer** (decoder-only) with linear read-in and read-out layers per modality, following the GATO / TDMs / Chameleon design lineage.

**Tokenization:**
- Neural data is tokenized by patching: a 20 ms time bin covering 32 channels forms one token (a vector of binned spike counts). An electrode array with 100 channels produces ceil(100/32) = 4 neural tokens per 20 ms timestep.
- Behavioral covariates are low-dimensional, so 1 token per behavioral dimension per 20 ms timestep.
- Additional special tokens per timestep: a **Phase token** (indicates whether behavior is driven by native body movement / BCI decoder / open-loop cue) and a **Return token** (encodes reward / future task completion quality, inspired by Decision Transformer).
- Fixed sequence length of 2 seconds (~100 timesteps), each yielding several tokens. Sequences with no spikes or covariate variability are discarded.

**Transformer:**
- Standard causal (unidirectional) Transformer.
- Tokens within a timestep are arranged in a canonical order: Phase, neural tokens, Return, behavioral tokens.
- **Rotary positional embeddings (RoPE)** track real-world timestamps.
- **Learned modality-specific position embeddings** distinguish token roles within a timestep.
- Trained autoregressively: each token predicts the next. At inference, Phase and Return tokens are omitted; behavioral tokens are zero-masked so the model predicts them from neural tokens.
- Two model sizes evaluated: **45M** and **350M** parameters.

**Objective:**
- **MSE** for continuous behavioral covariate prediction.
- **Cross-entropy** for spike count (neural reconstruction) and Return token prediction.
- **Covariate dropout** (key design choice): on 90% of training batches, a random fraction M ~ U[0,1] of covariate inputs is masked, and this masking is only applied after a random prefix T ~ U[0,2] seconds. This prevents the model from shortcutting through teacher-forced behavioral inputs (autoregressive collapse) and ensures it learns a genuine neural-to-behavior map.

### Pretraining Strategy

- Pretrained jointly on all ~2000 hours of paired neural and behavioral data from 30+ subjects.
- Data are cut into fixed 2-second non-overlapping windows, without explicit trial-boundary annotations, enabling GPU-efficient batching (similar to language modeling "packing").
- All behavioral kinematics are converted to velocities; every behavioral variable is normalized so its maximum absolute value is 1.
- Pretraining is stopped by early validation-loss stopping or a maximum of 400 epochs.
- The 45M model trained on 200 hours uses ~480 A100-hours; the 350M model trained on 2000 hours uses ~20,000 A100-hours.

### Cross-Subject / Cross-Task Transfer Mechanism

- **Shared backbone with per-modality linear read-ins and read-outs**: each dataset's electrode configuration is mapped into the shared embedding space via a linear layer. At evaluation time, a new read-in/read-out is initialized and the entire model is fine-tuned jointly on the target dataset.
- **Fine-tuning strategy**: NDT3 is fine-tuned jointly over all sessions of an evaluation dataset (not per-session), which improves capacity utilization and reflects realistic multi-session BCI deployment.
- **No frozen layers**: full fine-tuning updates all parameters. Task-specific parameters are not added; the output head is simply the appropriate behavioral read-out.
- **Cross-session transfer** works well: additional sessions from the same subject improve decoding, and this benefit persists after large-scale pretraining. **Cross-subject transfer** provides weaker benefit: pretrained models start at a performance level just slightly above the best from-scratch cross-subject performance but do not continue improving with more cross-subject data. This is attributed to sensor variability (different neurons, different channel-to-behavior mappings across subjects).

### Handling Input Order Sensitivity (Key Limitation)

- Cross-subject generalization is degraded by permuting the input channel order ("channel shuffle"), even a half-token shift in channel indexing is sufficient to suppress transfer. This means NDT3 has implicitly learned that token position encodes physical electrode location, and this assumption breaks across subjects with different electrode placements. Transformers can partially recover from token-level shuffles but not from sub-token spatial permutations.

## Key Results

### Aggregate decoding performance (8 tasks x 3-5 data scales, R^2)
| Model | Avg R^2 |
|---|---|
| Wiener Filter (linear) | ~0.20 |
| NDT2 (scratch) | ~0.19 |
| NDT2 100 hr pretrained | ~0.21 |
| NDT3 from scratch | ~0.23 |
| NDT3 45M 1.5 hr pretrain | ~0.27 |
| NDT3 45M 200 hr pretrain | ~0.30 |
| NDT3 45M 2 khr pretrain | ~0.30 (saturates at 45M) |
| NDT3 350M 2 khr pretrain | ~0.33 |

### When pretraining helps vs. does not
- **Helps clearly**: Low downstream data regimes (< 1.5 hours / < 5K data points). Even 1.5 hours of pretraining meaningfully lifts performance. Bimanual cursor task (complex, high-dim) benefits strongly at all data scales. Gains generalize across distribution shifts (time, pose, spring load) and to new brain areas (S1 and FEF/MT oculomotor cortex).
- **Pretraining gains vanish by 1.5-5K data points** for most tasks when comparing pretrained vs. from-scratch NDT3. This is weak by NLP/CV standards and implies pretraining provides relatively low information about any individual dataset's specifics.
- **Does not help**: Self-paced reach task shows minimal clear benefit from pretraining at any data scale (from-scratch NDT3 is competitive throughout). FALCON H2 handwriting task (sequence-to-sequence, CTC loss) shows no clear pretraining benefit and has unstable fine-tuning dynamics.
- **Scaling to 2 khr at 45M params degrades performance vs. 200 hr** (interference from dissimilar pretraining data). This is only mitigated by also scaling model size to 350M. Data scale and model scale must increase jointly.
- **Angular extrapolation fails**: NDT3 (all versions) cannot generalize to held-out reach angles outside the training distribution. A simple Wiener Filter + PCA-LDA can extrapolate to held-out angles, but NDT3 exploits attractor-like neural dynamics and produces predictions confined to the training distribution. This reflects "output stereotypy" -- a fundamental limitation of learning from repeated stereotyped behaviors.

### Scaling behavior
- 45M: benefits from increasing pretraining data up to 200 hours, then saturates or degrades at 2 khr.
- 350M: continues improving from 200 hr to 2 khr; is the only model where scaling to 2 khr provides significant benefit.
- Statistical significance (FDR-corrected t-tests): 350M 2 khr is significantly better (p < 0.06) over all other pretrained NDT3s except 350M 200 hr.
- Pretraining loss and downstream R^2 decouple: scaling the pretraining corpus alone does not monotonically improve downstream task performance unless model capacity is increased in parallel.

### Trial structure sensitivity
- From-scratch models overfit to trial boundaries (assume no motion before trial start). Pretrained NDT3 degrades more gracefully in continuous evaluation vs. trialized evaluation, showing reduced dependence on trial structure -- a property useful for online BCI control.

## Cross-Patient Relevance
**How does NDT3 apply to cross-patient speech decoding from intra-op uECOG (~20 patients, ~20 min each)?**

1. **Data regime is deeply unfavorable**: 20 patients x 20 min = ~6.7 hours total. NDT3's pretraining benefits are empirically limited to the regime where < 1.5 hours of downstream data is available per evaluation dataset. Beyond 1.5 hours (= 5K data points), from-scratch NDT3 matches pretrained NDT3 in most tasks. Your intra-op patients would each be in the ultra-low-data regime (20 min per patient), where pretraining is most valuable -- but the total corpus across patients is also tiny, making *pretraining from scratch* on the cross-patient uECOG corpus infeasible.

2. **Modality gap is significant**: NDT3 is built entirely on intracortical spike data from Utah arrays. uECOG records field potentials (high-gamma power, broadband envelope) from the cortical surface at much coarser spatial resolution. The tokenization scheme (32-channel patches of spike counts) and the priors learned from spiking dynamics do not transfer directly. A new pretraining corpus matching uECOG signal statistics would be needed, or a public ECoG dataset could be incorporated.

3. **Sensor variability is the binding constraint**: NDT3's cross-subject transfer is limited precisely because different electrode placements mean different neurons are recorded per subject. This problem is even more severe with uECOG, where electrode positioning varies substantially across intra-op patients and grids are typically removed after surgery. The per-subject read-in/read-out architecture (linear projection per patient) partially addresses this, but the paper shows that even sub-token channel permutations are sufficient to abolish cross-subject transfer in NDT3.

4. **Speech is a sequence-to-sequence task, not continuous regression**: NDT3's architecture predicts continuous low-dimensional behavioral covariates (kinematic velocities, forces) at each 20 ms timestep. Speech decoding to phonemes or words is a sequence-to-sequence mapping that NDT3 does not naturally support. The one experiment with a sequence-to-sequence task (handwriting, CTC loss) showed no benefit from pretraining and unstable training.

5. **Short sessions per patient are partially compatible with the framework**: The covariate dropout technique and joint multi-session fine-tuning strategy could be adapted to pool across multiple patients (treating each patient as a "session"). The per-modality read-in/read-out architecture natively handles varying electrode counts.

6. **Pretraining on public motor cortex data could still help**: If your model architecture is compatible, initializing from the NDT3 350M 2 khr checkpoint (publicly released) could provide better neural representations than random initialization, even for uECOG speech data -- though the benefit is likely small compared to what NDT3 achieves in its own domain.

## Limitations

- **Sensor variability limits cross-subject scaling**: Cross-subject pretraining benefits are low because input channel semantics differ per subject. The Transformer's positional encoding of channel identity is brittle to cross-subject permutation. This is arguably the central unsolved problem for foundation models in spiking neural data.
- **Output stereotypy limits generalization to novel behaviors**: Fine-tuned NDT3 cannot extrapolate beyond the output range seen during training. Tasks with stereotyped, repeated behaviors provide limited diversity for learning generalizable behavioral priors.
- **Scaling requires joint data + model growth**: Scaling pretraining data past 200 hours at fixed 45M parameters degrades performance. This means diminishing returns without also scaling compute, which is expensive.
- **Sequence-to-sequence tasks not supported**: The handwriting (CTC) task showed no benefit and unstable training. Speech decoding, typing, and other discrete symbolic output tasks are outside NDT3's current design.
- **Pretraining benefit is small by NLP/CV standards**: The threshold where pretraining advantage disappears is 1.5 hours (5K data points), far below the data scales needed in NLP/CV to match pretrained models from scratch.
- **Causal architecture vs. sensor variability trade-off**: The autoregressive design enables online BCI use, but cross-subject channel permutation sensitivity is a fundamental limitation that causal masking does not resolve.
- **Only motor cortex / closely related areas tested**: Gains in S1 and oculomotor cortex (FEF/MT) are encouraging but limited; applicability to language areas (IFG, STG, premotor) is untested.
- **Pretraining data is monkey-dominated**: Most of the 2000 hours comes from monkey experiments. Human data is scarce (all humans are held out from pretraining in the evaluation setup), limiting direct applicability to human BCIs.

## Reusable Ideas

1. **Per-modality linear read-in / read-out with a shared autoregressive backbone**: Each subject or electrode configuration gets its own linear projection into the shared token embedding space. This is the minimal, architecturally clean solution for handling variable electrode counts across patients without modifying the transformer. Directly applicable to cross-patient uECOG.

2. **Spatial patch tokenization (N channels per token)**: Grouping adjacent channels into a single token (32 channels per 20 ms bin) reduces sequence length, aligns spatial scale with population-level dynamics, and handles variable electrode counts through the read-in layer. For uECOG with coarser spatial resolution, patch sizes may need tuning, but the principle transfers.

3. **Covariate dropout as anti-shortcut regularization**: Masking a random fraction of behavioral inputs during training, applied only after a random prefix, forces the model to learn the neural-to-behavior map rather than fitting autocorrelated behavioral sequences. This is a lightweight technique applicable to any neural decoding model trained with teacher forcing, including speech decoders.

4. **Joint multi-session / multi-patient fine-tuning**: Fine-tuning on pooled data from multiple sessions (or multiple patients) outperforms per-session tuning. For intra-op uECOG where individual patients have only ~20 min, pooling across patients at fine-tuning time is an important strategy even if cross-patient pretraining benefits are small.

5. **BCI phase and return conditioning tokens**: Marking whether behavioral data is generated by native movement, BCI decoder, or open-loop cue, and providing a return signal for task completion, distinguishes the quality of behavioral supervision. For intra-op speech data where the patient is reading cued words, the phase token concept maps naturally to a "task cue" token that marks whether a given window is stimulus-aligned or spontaneous.

6. **Continuous (non-trialized) training and evaluation**: NDT3 cuts random contiguous windows from the recording without trial alignment. This avoids overfitting to trial structure and is directly applicable to intra-op speech recordings, which are often short and may not have clean trial boundaries. From-scratch models that learn trial structure overfit to it; pooling cross-patient data with random windowing prevents this.

7. **Rotary positional embeddings (RoPE) for real-world time**: RoPE tracks absolute recording time, enabling the model to represent temporal position across variable-length and multi-session sequences without relearning positional structure. For cross-patient data with different recording durations, this is cleaner than learned absolute position embeddings.

8. **Scaling model size jointly with data**: The key lesson from NDT3's scaling analysis is that data scale alone is insufficient -- model capacity must scale in tandem. For a cross-patient uECOG speech decoder, this implies that even a small pretraining corpus (e.g., pooled public ECoG datasets) is most valuable when model size is also increased beyond what task-specific data alone would justify.

9. **Distribution shift robustness as an evaluation axis**: NDT3 explicitly evaluates OOD generalization across time (1 min vs. 1 hr sessions), pose shifts, and spring load changes, showing pretrained models are more robust. For intra-op speech data, analogous evaluations would be: within-session vs. cross-session (different day), and cross-electrode-grid generalization. Structuring evaluation to measure these shifts provides a more informative signal than aggregate accuracy.
