# Pandarinath et al. 2018 - LFADS: Inferring Neural Population Dynamics

## Citation

Pandarinath, C., O'Shea, D. J., Collins, J., Jozefowicz, R., Stavisky, S. D., Kao, J. C., Trautmann, E. M., Kaufman, M. T., Ryu, S. I., Hochberg, L. R., Henderson, J. M., Shenoy, K. V., Abbott, L. F., & Sussillo, D. (2018). Inferring single-trial neural population dynamics using sequential auto-encoders. *Nature Methods*, 15(10), 805-815. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41592-018-0109-9

## Problem Statement

Standard neuroscience analyses average across trials to estimate neural population dynamics, sacrificing single-trial information. Recovering population dynamics on single trials is hard because of: (1) incomplete sampling of the neural population, (2) trial-to-trial variability in neural responses, and (3) fluctuations in action potential timing. Existing single-trial methods assume independent underlying factors (e.g., factor analysis), linear dynamics, or switching linear dynamics -- none of which capture the full nonlinear dynamical structure of neural population activity. The paper asks: can we infer de-noised, low-dimensional latent dynamics from single-trial spiking data by leveraging a nonlinear dynamical systems prior?

## Method

### Core Assumption

Neural population spiking activity is generated by an underlying continuous dynamical system: **x_dot(t) = F(x(t), u(t))**, where x(t) is the latent state, F is a nonlinear function, and u(t) is an optional external input. LFADS learns F, the mapping from latent factors to firing rates, and (optionally) infers inputs u(t) -- all from observed spike counts alone.

### Architecture

LFADS is a **sequential variational autoencoder (VAE)** with the following components:

**Encoder (bidirectional RNN):**
- Takes the full time series of binned spike counts for a single trial as input.
- Runs forward and backward in time, so at any time t it has access to past and future data.
- Compresses the entire trial into a single latent vector -- the "initial condition" g0 -- parameterized as a diagonal Gaussian (mean mu, log-variance sigma). This is the trial-specific initial state.
- A separate encoder pathway feeds into the Controller (see below).

**Generator (RNN, specifically GRU):**
- The core dynamical system. Seeded with the initial condition g0, it evolves forward in time to produce latent dynamics.
- At each timestep, outputs a state g_t, which is linearly mapped to low-dimensional **factors f_t** via W^fac.
- Factors are then mapped to **firing rates r_t** via: r_t = exp(W^rate * f_t) (exponential nonlinearity ensures positive rates).
- Observed spikes are modeled as Poisson draws from these rates: x_t ~ Poisson(x | r_t).
- The generator units do NOT correspond to individual neurons; they model abstract dynamics.

**Controller (RNN) -- optional, for inferring inputs:**
- A second bidirectional encoder (the "controller encoder") feeds time-varying information to a Controller RNN.
- At each timestep, the controller outputs inferred inputs u_t, drawn from a Gaussian with mean and variance parameterized by the controller state.
- u_t is fed into the generator, allowing it to model non-autonomous dynamics (external perturbations, inputs from other brain areas).
- The prior on u_t is an AR(1) process (autoregressive with lag 1), not white noise -- this encourages smooth, temporally correlated inferred inputs.
- The KL divergence on u_t acts as a regularizer: the system prefers to explain data via autonomous dynamics (g0 alone) unless inputs are truly needed.

**Dimensionality summary:**
- Observed data: N neurons x T timesteps (binned spike counts, typically 5-10 ms bins).
- Initial condition g0: low-dimensional (learned).
- Generator: higher-dimensional hidden state (e.g., 64-200 units).
- Factors f_t: low-dimensional (e.g., 40), constrained to be fewer than the number of neurons.
- Inferred inputs u_t: very low-dimensional (e.g., 1-4 dimensions).

### Training Procedure

- **Loss function:** Maximize a variational lower bound (ELBO) on log P(x). This combines:
  - Reconstruction term: sum of log Poisson likelihoods over all neurons and timesteps.
  - KL penalty on g0: keeps initial conditions close to a Gaussian prior (variance kappa = 0.1).
  - KL penalty on u_t: keeps inferred inputs close to the AR(1) prior.
- **KL annealing:** The KL divergence penalty is linearly increased over the first ~2000 training steps to avoid posterior collapse.
- **L2 regularization** on recurrent weight matrices (specifically on h_{t-1} terms) to encourage simple dynamics.
- **Dropout** on several connections (around g0, around the input to the generator, around the controller) to prevent overfitting.
- **Optimizer:** ADAM (lr=0.01, beta1=0.9, beta2=0.999, epsilon=0.1). Learning rate decayed by 0.95x when training error does not decrease, down to a minimum of 1e-5.
- **Gradient clipping** at a max norm of 200.
- **Train/validation split:** 4:1 ratio; validation data used for checkpointing only (not for updating weights).
- **Posterior averaging:** At inference time, the model is run multiple times (e.g., 512) with different samples of the stochastic latents, then averaged to get stable rate estimates.

### Dynamic Neural Stitching (Multi-Session Extension)

- For combining data across sessions with different recorded neurons: each session gets its own **read-in matrix** W^input (maps observed spikes to input factors) and **read-out matrix** W^rate_s (maps factors to session-specific firing rates).
- The encoder, generator, controller, and factor matrix W^fac are **shared** across all sessions.
- Read-in/read-out matrices initialized via PCA regression on condition-averaged firing rates.
- This forces a common latent dynamical model while accommodating different neuron identities per session.

## Key Results

1. **Simulated data validation:** LFADS outperformed GPFA, Poisson feed-forward neural network LDS (PfLDS), and variational latent Gaussian process (vLGP) on recovering ground-truth rates from chaotic RNN and Lorenz attractor simulations.

2. **Maze reaching task (monkey M1+PMd, 202 neurons, ~2300 trials, 108 conditions):**
   - LFADS-inferred rates decoded hand velocity with R^2 = 0.90 vs. 0.66 (smoothing), 0.69 (GPFA), 0.34 (binning).
   - LFADS outperformed alternatives even when using only 25 neurons (vs. 202 for competitors).
   - LFADS-inferred factors predicted held-out neurons better than GPFA (higher log-likelihood per spike).

3. **Single-trial rotational dynamics:** LFADS revealed rotational dynamics in M1 on single trials (previously only visible in condition-averaged data). Demonstrated in both macaque and human (BrainGate participant T5) motor cortex. The generator learned dynamics that **generalized to held-out reach conditions** it was never trained on.

4. **Dynamic neural stitching (44 sessions, monkey P, spanning 162 days):**
   - Stitched LFADS combined non-overlapping neuron populations across sessions into one coherent dynamical model.
   - Stitched model decoded kinematics with R^2 = 0.76 vs. 0.50 (single-session LFADS), 0.19 (GPFA), 0.10 (smoothing).
   - Factor trajectories were consistent across sessions for the same behavioral conditions.

5. **Inferring inputs (cursor jump task):** With a controller enabled (4 inferred input dimensions), LFADS correctly identified the presence, identity (left vs. right perturbation), and timing of unexpected cursor perturbations -- without any perturbation labels during training.

6. **LFP correlation:** LFADS-inferred rates captured high-frequency (15-40 Hz) oscillatory dynamics phase-locked to LFPs, even though LFP data was never provided to the model. This required the use of inferred inputs.

## Cross-Patient Relevance

How could LFADS-style approaches help with cross-patient alignment from uECOG for speech decoding?

**Dynamic neural stitching is directly analogous to the cross-patient problem.** In the paper, different recording sessions have entirely non-overlapping neuron populations but share underlying dynamics (reaching). For cross-patient speech decoding from uECOG:

- **Per-patient read-in/read-out matrices** could map each patient's unique electrode configuration (different electrode counts, placements, cortical anatomy) into a shared latent dynamical space, exactly as LFADS does for per-session matrices.
- **Shared generator** would learn canonical speech dynamics (articulatory trajectories, phoneme sequences) common across patients, while per-patient adaptor layers handle anatomical and recording variability.
- **The assumption that speech production is governed by low-dimensional dynamics** is well-supported -- articulatory movements are highly constrained, and speech motor cortex likely exhibits structured dynamics analogous to the rotational dynamics found in arm reaching.

**Specific opportunities:**

1. **Small-data patients benefit from large-data patients:** Stitching lets the generator learn from all patients simultaneously. A patient with only a few minutes of recorded speech would benefit from the dynamical prior learned across many patients, analogous to how single-session LFADS improved when stitched with 43 other sessions.
2. **Electrode drift / reimplantation:** If electrode configurations change over time within a patient, stitching can treat each configuration as a separate "session" with its own read-in/read-out, maintaining a stable latent space.
3. **Alignment without explicit anatomical registration:** The stitching approach does not require knowing which neurons correspond across sessions. It learns the correspondence implicitly through shared dynamics. This sidesteps the hard problem of aligning electrodes across patients with different cortical folding.

**Caveats for uECOG adaptation:**

- LFADS was designed for single-unit spiking data (Poisson observation model). uECOG signals are continuous (high-gamma power, broadband), requiring a **Gaussian observation model** instead.
- uECOG has far fewer channels (typically 128-256) than the neuron counts in this paper, but each channel aggregates activity from many neurons, so the effective signal may still be rich.
- The assumption of a shared dynamical system across patients is stronger than across sessions within one animal. Cross-patient variability in cortical organization for speech may be larger than cross-session variability in motor cortex for reaching. Domain-specific regularization or hierarchical priors may be needed.

## Reusable Ideas

1. **Sequential VAE with dynamical prior:** The core architecture -- bidirectional encoder compresses a trial to an initial condition, GRU generator evolves dynamics forward, low-dimensional factors mapped to observations via exponential readout -- is a general-purpose framework for any neural time-series data. Swap the Poisson likelihood for Gaussian to handle continuous signals like uECOG.

2. **Per-session read-in/read-out with shared dynamics:** The stitching architecture is the most directly transferable idea. Implement per-patient linear layers that project heterogeneous electrode configurations into a common latent space, with a shared nonlinear dynamics model.

3. **Inferred inputs via controller:** The controller mechanism for inferring unmeasured external inputs is valuable for speech decoding, where auditory feedback, linguistic planning signals, and other top-down inputs drive speech motor cortex but are not directly observed from motor electrodes alone.

4. **AR(1) prior on inferred inputs:** Rather than white noise, using a temporally smooth prior on inferred inputs is a simple but effective regularization. Applicable to any latent variable model where you expect smooth time-varying signals.

5. **KL annealing schedule:** Linearly ramping up the KL penalty over early training prevents posterior collapse in VAEs -- a practical trick that is broadly useful.

6. **Posterior averaging at inference:** Running the model multiple times with different latent samples and averaging gives more stable estimates than a single forward pass. Important when downstream decoding depends on these estimates.

7. **Factor dimensionality as a bottleneck:** Constraining the factor dimension to be much smaller than the neuron/channel count forces the model to learn a compressed, de-noised representation. This is a principled alternative to PCA-then-model pipelines.

8. **PCA-based initialization for stitching matrices:** Using PCA regression on condition-averaged data to initialize the per-session read-in and read-out matrices provides a warm start that significantly aids optimization. For cross-patient speech, could initialize per-patient matrices using PCA on phoneme-averaged high-gamma responses.

9. **Validation via held-out neurons:** Testing whether inferred factors predict the activity of neurons not used for training (Fig. 2f) is a strong validation strategy. For uECOG, hold out electrodes and check if the latent factors predict their signals.

10. **Code availability:** The original LFADS implementation is at https://github.com/tensorflow/models/tree/master/research/lfads, with a MATLAB interface at https://lfads.github.io. Subsequent implementations include `lfads-torch` and the AutoLFADS hyperparameter search framework.
