# Metzger et al. 2023 - Speech Decoding and Avatar Control Neuroprosthesis

## Citation

Metzger, S. L., Littlejohn, K. T., Silva, A. B., Moses, D. A., Seaton, M. P., Wang, R., Dougherty, M. E., Liu, J. R., Wu, P., Berger, M. A., Zhuravleva, I., Tu-Chan, A., Ganguly, K., Anumanchipalli, G. K., & Chang, E. F. (2023). A high-performance neuroprosthesis for speech decoding and avatar control. *Nature*, 620, 1037-1046. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-023-06443-4

## Setup (modality, patients, brain region, task, data amount)

- **Modality**: High-density surface ECoG (253-channel array, disc-shaped electrodes, 3 mm center-to-center spacing, 1 mm recording-contact diameter). Not uECoG -- standard subdural ECoG but at high density.
- **Patients**: Single participant (N=1), 47-year-old female with severe paralysis and anarthria from a basilar-artery brainstem stroke 18 years prior. Quadriplegia; cannot speak or type. Used head-tracking assistive device at ~14 WPM baseline.
- **Brain region**: Left hemisphere lateral cortex -- sensorimotor cortex (SMC) and superior temporal gyrus, covering precentral, postcentral, and superior/middle temporal gyri. Array centered over speech cortical areas for orofacial movements.
- **Task**: Prompted silent speech -- participant silently attempted to speak sentences shown on screen. Three sentence sets: 50-phrase-AAC (119 unique words), 529-phrase-AAC (372 unique words), 1024-word-General (9,655 sentences, 1,024 unique words from Twitter/film transcriptions). Also: NATO code-word classification task, articulatory-movement task (6 orofacial movements), emotional-expression task (3 emotions x 3 intensities).
- **Data amount**: ~13 days of data collection, ~9,506 sentence trials (~1.6 h of training data per day). Reached high performance within 14 days of data collection. Signal remained stable for 61+ days without recalibration.

## Method (preprocessing, decoder architecture, training)

### Preprocessing
- Extracted high-gamma activity (HGA; 70-150 Hz) and low-frequency signals (LFS; 0.3-17 Hz) from ECoG at 200 Hz sampling rate.
- Common average referencing, downsampled to 1 kHz, anti-aliasing filter at 500 Hz cutoff.
- HGA: analytic amplitude via bandpass, then downsampled to 200 Hz. LFS: lowpass at 100 Hz, downsampled to 200 Hz.
- z-scored HGA and LFS with 30-s sliding window per channel.
- For text decoding: further downsampled by factor of 6 (to 33.33 Hz), L2-normalized per channel.

### Decoder Architecture
Three parallel output modalities, each with its own decoder:

**1. Text decoding (ECoG -> phones -> text)**
- Bidirectional RNN: 1D conv layer (500 kernels, size 4, stride 4) followed by 4 layers of bidirectional GRU (500 hidden units each). Linear projection to 41D (39 phones + silence + CTC blank).
- Trained with CTC loss (no need for temporal alignment of neural activity to phone labels).
- CTC beam search decodes phone probabilities into words using a lexicon (g2p-en for pronunciations).
- 5-gram language model (Kneser-Ney smoothing) trained on Twitter + Cornell film corpus sentences, applied during beam search.
- 8-s window of neural activity (1 s before to 7.5 s after go cue) at inference; early stopping via silence detection.

**2. Speech synthesis (ECoG -> discrete speech units -> audio)**
- Bidirectional RNN: 1D conv (260 kernels, width 6, stride 6), 3 layers bidirectional GRU (hidden dim 260), 1D transpose conv. Outputs probability of 100 discrete speech units (derived from HuBERT) + CTC blank at each time step (every 5 ms).
- Reference speech-unit sequences generated by applying HuBERT to basis waveforms (recruited speaker for AAC sets; Wavenet TTS for 1024-word-General).
- Predicted units passed to a pretrained unit-to-speech model (mel spectrogram) then vocoded to audio in real time.
- Offline voice personalization: YourTTS zero-shot voice conversion conditioned on a pre-injury speech clip. Real-time personalization via HiFi-CAR convolutional network.
- SpecAugment applied during training for robustness.

**3. Avatar animation (ECoG -> articulatory gestures -> face animation)**
- **Direct approach**: Bidirectional RNN decodes neural activity into discretized articulatory gestures (quantized by VQ-VAE with 40-unit codebook). VQ-VAE decoder reconstructs continuous gestures. Gesture-to-animation system (Speech Graphics) drives a 3D MetaHuman avatar (Unreal Engine 4.26).
- **Acoustic approach**: Uses decoded speech audio from the synthesis pipeline, passed through a speech-to-gesture model to generate avatar animation. Lower latency; used for real-time testing.
- Articulatory gestures: 16 speech-related gestures (10 lips, 4 tongue, 1 jaw, 1 nostril).
- Non-speech tasks: feedforward MLP for articulatory-movement classification (6 movements); small RNN for emotional-expression classification (9 expressions = 3 emotions x 3 intensities).

### Training
- CTC loss used across all three main decoders (text, synthesis, avatar direct) -- key design choice enabling training without temporal alignment.
- Transfer learning for NATO code-word classifier: initialized from pretrained weights, replaced input/output layers.
- Training data: 95% training / 5% held-out development set.
- Data augmentation: temporal jitter (randomly sampling 8-s windows from 9-s spans during training), SpecAugment for synthesis model.
- Models implemented in PyTorch.

## Key Results (quantitative)

### Text Decoding (1024-word-General set, real-time)
- **WER: 25.5%** (median, 99% CI [19.3, 34.5]) -- below the 30% usability threshold for speech recognition
- **PER: 18.5%** (median, 99% CI [14.1, 28.5])
- **CER: 19.9%** (median, 99% CI [15.0, 30.1])
- **Decoding speed: 78.3 WPM** (median, 99% CI [75.5, 79.4]) -- 5x faster than participant's existing assistive device (14.2 WPM)
- Vocabulary scales robustly: offline WER of 27.6% even at 39,378-word vocabulary
- Neural decoding alone (no LM) achieved 29.4% PER, showing the RNN is the primary driver
- Performance improved steadily over 13 days of data collection
- NATO code-word classifier: 96.8% accuracy (frozen after day 40, stable across 19 sessions over 61 days)

### AAC Sentence Sets
- 50-phrase-AAC: WER 4.92% (median), 101 WPM decoding speed
- 529-phrase-AAC: WER 17.1% (median), 89.9 WPM decoding speed

### Speech Synthesis
- **MCD** (mel-cepstral distortion, lower = better): 3.45, 4.49, 5.21 dB for 50-phrase-AAC, 529-phrase-AAC, 1024-word-General respectively
- MCDs comparable to text-to-speech synthesizers and better than previous neural-decoding speech synthesis
- **Perceptual WER** (human listeners): 8.20%, 28.2%, 54.4% for the three sets
- **Perceptual CER**: 6.64%, 26.3%, 45.7% for the three sets
- All significantly better than chance (P < 0.0001)

### Avatar Animation
- **Perceptual accuracy** (crowd-sourced forced-choice): 85.7%, 87.7%, 74.3% across the three sentence sets
- Articulatory-movement classification: 87.8% median accuracy (6 movements, 10-fold CV)
- Emotional-expression classification: 74.0% median accuracy (9 expressions, 15-fold CV); 96.9% for 3 coarse emotions
- Decoded avatar facial landmarks correlated with healthy speakers (jaw: r=0.733, lip aperture: r=0.690, mouth width: r=0.446)

### Articulatory Encoding
- Articulatory representations persist in SMC 18 years after paralysis onset
- Phone encodings organized somatotopically by place of articulation (POA): labial, front tongue, back tongue, vocalic (P < 0.0001)
- Strong correlation between phonetic tuning and non-speech articulatory-movement activations (tongue: r=0.84, lips: r=0.89)
- Electrodes encoding speech were distributed broadly across the SMC; most critical electrodes were in SMC

## Cross-Patient Relevance

- **Single-patient limitation acknowledged**: Results from N=1; the authors explicitly state that validating across individuals with varying degrees and etiologies of paralysis is the key next step.
- **Articulatory representations are the foundation**: Decoding performance is driven by articulatory (not acoustic) representations in SMC that are somatotopically organized and persist after years of paralysis. This is encouraging for cross-patient approaches because articulatory representations should share structure across individuals.
- **Broad cortical coverage matters**: Performance was driven by distributed electrode coverage across the lateral SMC, not a small focal region. Leave-one-area-out analyses confirmed SMC was most critical. This implies cross-patient models need broad coverage or a way to align across electrode placements.
- **No acoustic ground truth needed**: CTC loss eliminates the need for temporal alignment between neural activity and output labels. This is critical for paralyzed patients who produce no audible speech. Any cross-patient approach must similarly handle the lack of ground-truth speech.
- **Signal stability**: Cortical-surface (non-penetrating) electrodes showed stable signals over 61+ days without recalibration, which is relevant for long-term cross-patient deployments.
- **High-density ECoG vs. uECoG**: This study uses standard subdural ECoG (253 channels, 3 mm spacing), not micro-ECoG. The spatial scale and signal characteristics differ from uECoG. Cross-patient transfer between the two modalities would require handling resolution differences.

## Reusable Ideas

1. **CTC loss for speech decoding without alignment**: The single most impactful design choice. CTC loss allows training phone/speech-unit decoders directly on sentence-level labels without frame-by-frame alignment. Essential for any silently-attempted-speech paradigm. Implemented via torchaudio's `ctc_decode`.

2. **Phone-level intermediate representation**: Decoding to phones (not words directly) enables vocabulary-independent neural decoding. The vocabulary constraint is applied only at the beam-search/language-model stage. This separation means the neural decoder generalizes across vocabulary sizes.

3. **HuBERT discrete speech units as synthesis targets**: Rather than predicting spectrograms directly, they predict discrete speech units from HuBERT (self-supervised, 100 units). This provides a compact, learnable intermediate for CTC training and enables unit-to-speech synthesis. Worth exploring for cross-patient speech synthesis.

4. **VQ-VAE for articulatory gesture quantization**: Discretizing continuous articulatory gestures via VQ-VAE (40-unit codebook) enables CTC-based training for the avatar decoder. The quantize-then-dequantize pattern is generally useful for turning continuous targets into CTC-compatible discrete sequences.

5. **Dual-feature extraction (HGA + LFS)**: Using both high-gamma activity (70-150 Hz) and low-frequency signals (0.3-17 Hz) from ECoG. Both contributed to decoding. Standard preprocessing pipeline worth adopting.

6. **5-gram language model with beam search**: Simple but effective. Trained on Twitter + film corpora for naturalistic language. The language model provided modest gains on top of the already-strong neural decoder (PER went from 29.4% to 18.5% with full pipeline).

7. **Architecture**: 1D conv front-end (for temporal downsampling and local feature extraction) followed by bidirectional GRU layers is the core pattern used across all three decoders. Conv kernel size and stride set to achieve desired temporal downsampling factor.

8. **Temporal jitter augmentation**: During training, randomly sample 8-s windows from 9-s neural activity spans around each trial. Simple augmentation that improves robustness to temporal variability.

9. **SpecAugment on ECoG features**: Applied during speech-synthesis model training. Originally designed for ASR; effective for neural-signal augmentation too.

10. **Electrode contribution analysis**: Computed gradient-based contribution scores (L1 norm of loss derivative w.r.t. HGA and LFS of each electrode, averaged across time and trials). Useful for interpretability and potentially for electrode selection in cross-patient settings.

11. **Receptive-field encoding model**: Linear temporal receptive-field model mapping phone-emission probabilities to HGA at each electrode. Produces per-electrode phone-encoding vectors that reveal somatotopic organization. Could be used to align electrode spaces across patients.

12. **Early stopping via silence detection**: During real-time inference, the decoder monitors for sustained silence predictions (88.8% average probability over 8 recent time steps) to end decoding before the full trial window expires. Practical for variable-length utterances.
