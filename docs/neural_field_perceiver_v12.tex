\section{Problem}\label{sec:problem}

Each patient's $\mu$ECoG array is a sparse spatial sample of cortical high-gamma activity (HGA, 70--150\,Hz, 200\,Hz) during speech. The array sits on left ventral sensorimotor cortex (vSMC), and electrode positions in MNI-152 space are determined post-hoc from surgical reconstruction.

\textbf{Hardware.} LCP thin-film arrays (Dyconex~\cite{duraivel2023}), physically flexible --- they conform to cortical curvature. Two configurations:

\begin{table}[ht!]\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
& \textbf{128-ch (DBS)} & \textbf{256-ch (craniotomy)} \\
\midrule
Grid & $8 \times 16$ & $12 \times 22$ \\
Pitch & 1.33\,mm & 1.72\,mm \\
Footprint & ${\sim}11 \times 21$\,mm & ${\sim}21 \times 38$\,mm \\
Contact diameter & 200\,$\mu$m & 200\,$\mu$m \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Task.} Non-word repetition (intra-operative, no delay --- patients repeat immediately after hearing stimulus): 52 CVC/VCV tokens, 9 phonemes, 3 positions per token. Output: three 9-way classifications per trial. Epochs are per-phoneme, locked to each phoneme's production onset via MFA ($t_{\min}{=}{-}0.15$, $t_{\max}{=}0.5$\,s). MFA alignment is good for 8/11 patients; 3 patients (S16, S22, S39) have 300--500\,ms offsets and use full-trial fallback.

\textbf{Cross-patient challenge.} Arrays are offset 15--25\,mm in MNI space with variable rotation. Channel indices are meaningless across patients. Current LOPO achieves PER\,=\,0.762 (S14, grouped-by-token CV) using a per-patient Conv2d read-in that ignores electrode coordinates entirely. 55 architecture/training experiments converge to PER 0.750--0.780, suggesting a systematic barrier.

\textbf{Constraints.}

\begin{table}[ht!]\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}llr@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Constraint} & \textbf{Value} & \textbf{Impact} \\
\midrule
Sig.\ electrodes/patient & 63--201 & Sparse cortical sampling \\
Cross-patient offset & 15--25\,mm & Different cortex sampled \\
Trials/patient & 46--178 & Small-data regime \\
Utterance/patient & ${\sim}$1\,min & SSL has failed \\
Patients & ${\sim}$10 & Too few for large models \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Why coordinates might help.} The current pipeline treats each patient's electrode array as an opaque tensor. Per-patient Conv2d learns spatial filters independently per patient --- nothing is spatially shared. If two patients have electrodes sampling the same cortical region, the model cannot exploit this. MNI coordinates make spatial correspondence explicit: electrodes at similar brain positions should carry similar information, regardless of which patient they belong to.

\section{Idea: Spatially-Grounded Electrode Encoding}\label{sec:idea}

\subsection{What we assume}

Speech motor cortex has \textbf{conserved somatotopic organization}: the mapping from brain position to articulatory function is approximately shared across people. Gross somatotopic ordering (medial$\to$lateral: leg$\to$hand$\to$face) is essentially invariant~\cite{roux2020}. Fine structure varies --- speech motor cortex shows the highest positional variability (${\sim}$15--25\,mm~\cite{eichert2020}) and morphological diversity (five distinct sulcal types in the subcentral region). But functional connectivity is the most conserved feature of sensorimotor cortex~\cite{mueller2013}. The spatial map exists; its absolute position varies.

\textcolor{BrickRed}{\textit{Untested:}} That this conservation is strong enough, at $\mu$ECoG resolution, to transfer across $N{=}10$ patients with centimeter-scale positional uncertainty. This is the central empirical question.

\subsection{The three components}\label{sec:components}

The idea has three parts, all spatial:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Spatial tokenization.} Each electrode becomes a token whose spatial identity comes from its MNI coordinates (via Fourier positional encoding), not its channel index.

\item \textbf{Spatial aggregation.} Cross-attention from fixed virtual electrodes to real electrodes, with a distance bias in the attention logits. Electrodes near a virtual electrode contribute more. This maps variable electrode counts to a fixed shared spatial frame.

\item \textbf{Spatial supervision.} An auxiliary loss that reconstructs each electrode's observed activity from the latent representation and the electrode's coordinates. This forces the latent to be spatially meaningful --- if you know \textit{where} an electrode is, you should be able to predict \textit{what} it sees.
\end{enumerate}

These three components are coupled: spatial supervision only makes sense because tokens carry spatial identity, and spatial aggregation is what gives the latent representation a spatial structure to supervise against. Remove any one and the others lose their purpose.

Everything else in the architecture --- temporal attention, classification head, training protocol --- is standard.

\subsection{What the patient-specific components are}

Per patient $i$:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item $\Delta^{(i)} \in \R^3$, $\omega^{(i)} \in \R^3$: translation and rotation of MNI coordinates (6 params). Absorbs systematic registration error. L2-regularized ($10^{-3}$), init at $\mathbf{0}$.
\item $W^{(i)} \in \R^{d \times d}$, $b^{(i)} \in \R^d$: per-patient input projection ($d^2 + d$ params). Applied to each electrode token after shared Conv1d temporal binning. Absorbs impedance, gain, tissue conductivity, and electrode-surface coupling differences. Initialized to identity + zero bias so the model starts at the shared solution. \textbf{Motivated by:} every successful cross-patient system has per-patient input capacity (BIT: ${\sim}$6M/pt Conv2d; Willett: 65.8K/day affine; Levin: 262K/session; Singh: per-patient Conv1D; seegnificant: per-subject heads are the \#1 ablation component at $\Delta R^2 = -0.18$).
\item $e^{(i)} \in \R^{64}$: patient embedding entering \textbf{only} the reconstruction decoder. During training, dropped to $\mathbf{0}$ with $p{=}0.5$ so the decoder learns to reconstruct without patient identity (matching zero-shot test conditions).
\end{itemize}

Total at $d{=}64$: ${\sim}$4.2K learnable params per patient (6 geometric + 4{,}160 input projection + 64 decoder embedding). The encoder sees patient identity \textit{only} through the input projection --- electrode coordinates and activity are otherwise processed by the shared backbone.

\textcolor{BrickRed}{\textit{Capacity calibration:}} 4.2K/pt $\times$ 10 patients = 42K per-patient params total, ${\sim}$37\% of the shared backbone at $B{=}1$. This is far below BIT (6M/pt) and Willett (65.8K/day) but appropriate for our 10-patient, 1{,}300-trial regime. A15 ablates this down to 70 params/pt to test whether the projection is needed or whether coordinates alone suffice.

\textcolor{BrickRed}{\textit{Patient identity in encoder:}} The per-patient projection $W^{(i)}$ gives the encoder explicit patient identity, but only in the form of a signal normalization (identity-initialized). This matches field consensus (BIT, Willett, Singh, seegnificant all use per-patient input layers). At $N{=}10$, electrode positions alone trivially identify patients anyway; the projection simply provides a principled pathway for absorbing measurement variation. The reconstruction decoder also receives $e^{(i)}$ for impedance-dependent reconstruction; $e$ is dropped to $\mathbf{0}$ with $p{=}0.5$ during training.

\subsection{Electrode tokens}\label{sec:tokens}

For electrode $j$ on patient $i$, at temporal bin $t'$:
\begin{equation}
\text{token}_j(t') = \underbrace{W^{(i)} \text{Conv1d}(d_j;\; k{=}10,\, s{=}10)_{t'} + b^{(i)}}_{\text{per-patient projected activity}} + \underbrace{\text{Linear}\bigl(\gamma(\tilde{\bfp}_j)\bigr)}_{\text{position (constant)}} + \underbrace{\text{sinPE}(t')}_{\text{temporal index}}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Temporal binning.} Per-electrode Conv1d($1 \to d$, $k{=}10$, $s{=}10$): non-overlapping 50\,ms windows at 200\,Hz $\to$ $T' \approx 20$ tokens. Shared weights across all electrodes and patients. Matches field standard (Spalding, Willett: 20\,Hz effective rate). Our BiGRU experiments show stride\,=\,5 (40\,Hz) $\approx$ stride\,=\,10 (20\,Hz); test as ablation.

\textbf{Per-patient input projection.} The shared Conv1d output is projected through a per-patient affine: $W^{(i)} x + b^{(i)}$, $W^{(i)} \in \R^{d \times d}$, initialized to identity. This absorbs per-patient gain, impedance, and tissue differences before the shared backbone sees the tokens. Functionally analogous to Willett's per-day affine layer and BIT's per-subject read-in.

\textbf{Fourier PE ($F{=}3$).} Calibrated MNI coordinates $\tilde{\bfp}_j = R(\omega^{(i)}) \bfp_j + \Delta^{(i)}$, standardized so the speech motor region spans $[-1, 1]$ per axis:
\begin{equation}
\gamma(\bar{\bfp}) = \bigl[\sin(2^0 \pi \bar{\bfp}),\; \cos(2^0 \pi \bar{\bfp}),\; \ldots,\; \sin(2^{2} \pi \bar{\bfp}),\; \cos(2^{2} \pi \bar{\bfp})\bigr] \in \R^{18}
\end{equation}
Wavelengths: 30\,mm (full-array), 15\,mm (articulatory region), 7.5\,mm (sub-region). Projected via Linear($18 \to d$).

\textbf{Why $F{=}3$.} Intraoperative MNI coordinates carry ${\sim}5$--$10$\,mm RMS error from stacking sources (navigation 2--4\,mm, brain shift 2--10\,mm, normalization 2--3\,mm). The finest wavelength at $F{=}3$ is 7.5\,mm --- just above the noise floor. $F{=}4$ (3.75\,mm) would encode noise.

\textbf{Geometric calibration} ($\Delta^{(i)}, \omega^{(i)}$, 6 params/patient). The registration system performs one calibration per patient; that systematic bias propagates to all electrodes. Rigid correction absorbs this dominant error mode. Non-rigid brain shift (${\sim}2$--$10$\,mm, spatially smooth) is handled by soft attention --- a few mm of position error still places electrodes in approximately the correct virtual electrode neighborhood.


\subsection{Virtual electrodes}\label{sec:virtual-electrodes}

$L = 16$ query vectors at \textbf{fixed} MNI positions, with learned query content vectors. Each virtual electrode asks ``what is the neural activity near this brain position?'' via cross-attention to all real electrodes. Output: $s(t) \in \R^{L \times d}$ --- a fixed-size spatial representation regardless of electrode count.

\textbf{Why virtual electrodes.} Patient A has 63 electrodes; Patient B has 201, at different positions. The downstream decoder needs a fixed-size input that means the same thing across patients. Virtual electrodes provide this: they query the brain at the \textbf{same 16 anatomical positions} for every patient, and the soft attention handles the fact that different real electrodes are nearby for each patient.

\textbf{Positions.} Fixed at Brainnetome atlas~\cite{fan2016} centroids of speech motor subregions (lip M1, tongue M1, jaw M1, laryngeal M1, lip S1, tongue S1, ventral premotor, SMA, etc.). Not learned --- at $N{=}10$, the atlas (derived from hundreds of subjects) is a better population average than anything gradient descent could find. Inter-patient anatomical variability (${\sim}15$--$25$\,mm) and MNI error (${\sim}5$--$10$\,mm) are both handled by the \textbf{soft spatial attention}, not by moving query positions: the Gaussian distance bias decays smoothly, so a virtual electrode at ``atlas tongue M1'' still attends to real electrodes 10--15\,mm away. Spacing: ${\sim}5$\,mm, matching somatotopic scale.


\subsection{Spatiotemporal blocks ($\times B$)}\label{sec:blocks}

Each block alternates:

\textbf{(a) Spatial cross-attention} (per time bin). Virtual electrodes (Q) attend to real electrode tokens (K, V):
\begin{equation}
A_{ij} = \frac{Q_i \cdot K_j}{\sqrt{d_h}} \;-\; \alpha_h \cdot \|\bar{\bfp}_i - \bar{\bfp}_j\|^2
\end{equation}
Standard scaled dot-product attention plus a learnable isotropic distance bias $\alpha_h > 0$ per head (4 heads, init 1.0). The distance term makes attention spatially local by default; content similarity can override for distant but functionally related electrodes.

\textbf{(b) Temporal self-attention} (per virtual electrode). Each of 16 virtual electrodes attends across ${\sim}20$ time bins. 4 heads, sinusoidal temporal PE. Captures articulatory dynamics.

\textbf{Block internals.} Pre-LN residual connections, ReLU FFN ($d_\text{ff} = 4d$), dropout $p{=}0.2$, DropPath $p{=}0.1$.

\textbf{Electrode masking (spatial).} During supervised training, 50\% of electrodes are excluded from KV for all time steps. The reconstruction loss is still computed at masked positions, forcing spatial interpolation.

\textbf{Temporal masking (SSL only).} For self-supervised pretraining (Section~\ref{sec:ssl}), contiguous spans of temporal patches are replaced with a learnable mask token. This forces the model to predict neural activity at masked time positions from surrounding context --- learning temporal dynamics rather than spatial interpolation. Motivated by BIT~\cite{zhang2026bit}, which showed temporal masking is the appropriate SSL objective for speech decoding. Both masking types can coexist: spatial masking for reconstruction loss, temporal masking for SSL.


\subsection{Reconstruction decoder}\label{sec:recon-decoder}

Predicts each electrode's activity from the latent state and the electrode's position.

\textbf{Position-weighted aggregation.} Electrode $j$ gets a local latent vector by weighting virtual electrode outputs by spatial proximity:
\begin{equation}
s_j^*(t) = \sum_{\ell=1}^{L} \text{softmax}_\ell\!\bigl(-\alpha_{\text{dec}} \|\bar{\tilde{\bfp}}_j - \bar{\text{v}}_\ell\|^2\bigr) \cdot s_\ell(t)
\end{equation}

\textbf{Prediction.} Linear projection per time bin:
\begin{equation}
\hat{d}_j^{(i)}(t) = w(e^{(i)}, \tilde{\bfp}_j)^\top s_j^*(t) + b(e^{(i)}, \tilde{\bfp}_j)
\end{equation}
where $w, b$ come from a small MLP($[\tilde{\bfp}_j;\; e^{(i)}] \to \R^{d+1}$), 2 layers, ${\sim}$5K params total. During training, $e^{(i)} \to \mathbf{0}$ with $p{=}0.5$.

\textbf{Reconstruction loss.}
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}} = \frac{1}{N_i T'} \sum_{j,t'} \bigl(d_j(t') - \hat{d}_j(t')\bigr)^2
\end{equation}
Computed over all electrodes including masked ones. Gradient flows back through the encoder, providing the spatial supervision signal.


\subsection{Phoneme decoder}\label{sec:phoneme-decoder}

Mean-pool across $L$ virtual electrodes: $s_\text{pool}(t') = \frac{1}{L}\sum_\ell s_\ell(t')$.

\textbf{Per-phoneme MFA input (default).} Each trial is split into 3 per-phoneme epochs locked to MFA onset ($t_\text{min}{=}{-}0.15$, $t_\text{max}{=}0.5$\,s). Each epoch yields a single 9-way classification. Temporal readout: global mean pool $\to$ linear head. This triples the labeled samples and anchors each label to the correct temporal context.

\textbf{Empirical support:} Per-phoneme MFA beats learned temporal attention by 6\,pp (PER 0.734 vs.\ 0.797) and wins on 8/11 patients (+4.0\,pp population mean). The 3 patients where full-trial wins (S16, S22, S39) have poor MFA alignment (300--500\,ms offsets); these patients use full-trial fallback. \textbf{A21} ablates full-trial with learned attention queries as an alternative.

\textbf{Trial-aware batching.} Consecutive per-phoneme epochs from the same trial share ${\sim}$85\% temporal overlap (mean inter-phoneme gap: 80--130\,ms). Batches are stratified so that epochs from the same trial do not appear in both train and validation within a fold. This prevents leakage from temporal autocorrelation.

Per position, dual head:
\begin{equation}
\text{logits}_p = \frac{1}{2}\bigl(\text{Linear}(\bfz_p \to 9) + \tau \cdot \text{normalize}(\text{Linear}(\bfz_p \to 15)) \cdot A^\top\bigr)
\end{equation}
$A \in \R^{9 \times 15}$: fixed articulatory feature matrix. $\tau$: learnable temperature.

$\mathcal{L}_\text{class} = \text{FocalCE}(\text{logits}_p, y_p;\; \gamma{=}2,\; \text{LS}{=}0.1)$ with mixup ($\alpha{=}0.2$).

\section{Training and Evaluation}\label{sec:training}

\subsection{Phase 1: Source training}

$N{-}1$ source patients. AdamW ($10^{-3}$), geometric params ($\Delta, \omega$, virtual positions) at $3 \times 10^{-4}$. Cosine decay with 20-epoch warmup. Weight decay $10^{-4}$, grad clip 5.0, batch 16. Early stopping on 20\% held-out source validation, patience 7.

\subsection{Phase 2: Target adaptation}

Per fold of 5-fold grouped-by-token CV on target patient:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Linear probe.} Freeze encoder. Train decoder + $\Delta^{(\text{tgt})} + \omega^{(\text{tgt})} + e^{(\text{tgt})}$. 150 epochs.
\item \textbf{Fine-tune.} Unfreeze encoder at $0.1\times$ LR. 100 epochs, patience 7.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Evaluation}

\textbf{Zero-shot:} Phase 1 only, $\Delta{=}\omega{=}e{=}\mathbf{0}$. Pure coordinate generalization.

\textbf{LP-FT (primary):} Phase 1 $\to$ Phase 2. Matches clinical calibration.

\textbf{Metrics:} PER (edit distance), grouped-by-token 5-fold CV, 3 seeds. Full recipe: weighted $k$-NN ($k{=}10$), TTA 16, dual head.

\section{Self-Supervised Pretraining}\label{sec:ssl}

SSL pretraining on continuous (unlabeled) neural recordings addresses the fundamental data bottleneck: ${\sim}$1{,}300 labeled trials vs.\ 456\,min of raw continuous EDF across 29 patients. The design below is informed by BIT~\cite{zhang2026bit} (ICLR 2026, SOTA on Brain-to-Text with 367\,h pretraining) and adapted to our $\mu$ECoG regime.

\subsection{Why temporal masking, not spatial}

Our supervised reconstruction loss (Section~\ref{sec:recon-decoder}) masks \textit{electrodes} and predicts spatial activity --- this teaches the model to interpolate across space. For SSL, we instead mask \textit{time} and predict temporal activity. The rationale:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Speech is temporal.} Phonemes are articulatory gestures evolving over ${\sim}$50--200\,ms. A model that predicts ``what happens next in this neural sequence'' learns speech dynamics. A model that predicts ``what's the activity at a masked electrode'' learns spatial smoothness --- useful but not speech-specific.
\item \textbf{BIT validates this.} BIT uses temporal span masking (up to 300\,ms) with MSE reconstruction on 367\,h of Utah array data. The pretrained encoder achieves SOTA PER (14.39\% T12) and transfers to imagined speech. Supervised cross-subject pretraining \textit{fails} (Appendix M), but SSL cross-subject pretraining helps.
\item \textbf{The two objectives complement.} Spatial reconstruction (supervised, Section~\ref{sec:recon-decoder}) teaches cross-patient electrode alignment. Temporal reconstruction (SSL, this section) teaches speech dynamics. Both can coexist in v12.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{SSL objective: masked temporal patch reconstruction}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Input.} Continuous HGA at 200\,Hz, windowed into ${\sim}$2\,s segments (400 samples). The same tokenization as supervised: per-electrode shared Conv1d $\to$ per-patient input projection $\to$ $d$-dimensional electrode tokens per time step.
\item \textbf{Temporal patching.} Group every $P{=}10$ time steps (50\,ms) into one patch $\to$ $T'{=}40$ patches/segment. Each patch token: $d$-dim mean over $P$ frames per electrode.
\item \textbf{Span masking.} Replace contiguous spans of patches with a learnable mask token $m \in \R^d$, maintaining a target mask ratio of 50\%. Maximum span length: 6 patches (300\,ms). Following BIT, masked spans are drawn to maintain the overall ratio while allowing variable-length spans.
\item \textbf{Encoder.} v12's spatial cross-attention + temporal self-attention blocks process the partially masked sequence (both masked and unmasked tokens).
\item \textbf{Reconstruction.} A reversed linear projection maps encoder outputs back to original patch dimensionality. MSE loss on masked positions only:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{SSL}} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{M}|} \sum_{t' \in \mathcal{M}} \|x_{t'} - \hat{x}_{t'}\|^2
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{M}$ is the set of masked patch indices.
\item \textbf{Validation.} $R^2$ on held-out reconstruction (not downstream PER) for checkpoint selection during pretraining.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Per-patient layers during SSL}

Following BIT, each patient gets \textbf{separate read-in and read-out layers} for SSL, while the transformer backbone is shared. This is critical: different patients have different electrode layouts, different numbers of channels, different impedance profiles. The per-patient input projection (Section~\ref{sec:tokens}, $W^{(i)}x + b^{(i)}$) absorbs these differences before the shared backbone sees the data.

\textcolor{BrickRed}{\textit{Architectural consistency note:}} BIT uses per-patient Conv2d on raw grids. Our supervised v12 uses shared per-electrode Conv1d + per-patient Linear projection (enabling coordinate-based tokenization). For SSL, we keep the same architecture: shared Conv1d $\to$ per-patient projection $\to$ Fourier PE $\to$ shared blocks. This ensures clean weight transfer from SSL to supervised. The per-patient SSL reconstruction head (Linear($d \to d_{\text{patch}}$)) is the only component discarded at transfer.

\subsection{Data augmentation for SSL}\label{sec:ssl-augment}

BIT uses heavier augmentation during pretraining than typical supervised training. Our existing augmentation module (\texttt{augmentation.py}) provides the right primitives, but SSL-specific tuning is needed:

\begin{table}[ht!]\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Augmentation} & \textbf{Supervised default} & \textbf{SSL recommendation} \\
\midrule
White noise (additive) & \texttt{noise\_frac}$\,{=}\,$0.02 & \textbf{0.10--0.20} (BIT: SD=0.2) \\
Constant offset (per-trial) & --- & \textbf{0.05--0.10} (BIT: SD=0.05; Willett: 0.2) \\
Amplitude scaling (per-electrode) & \texttt{amp\_scale\_std}$\,{=}\,$0.15 & \textbf{0.15--0.25} \\
Time shift & \texttt{time\_shift\_frames}$\,{=}\,$20 & \textbf{0} (continuous data, no onset) \\
Channel dropout & \texttt{channel\_dropout\_max}$\,{=}\,$0.2 & \textbf{0.1} (less aggressive; temporal masking is the main regularizer) \\
Gaussian smoothing (temporal) & --- & \textbf{width=2.0} (BIT uses this; smooths high-freq noise) \\
Temporal stretch & Disabled & \textbf{Disabled} (continuous data, no utterance structure) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Key differences from supervised augmentation:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{No time shift.} SSL operates on continuous windows, not aligned epochs. Random windowing provides implicit time shift.
\item \textbf{Heavier noise.} SSL must learn robust representations from noisy data. BIT uses $5{-}10\times$ more noise than our supervised default.
\item \textbf{Add constant offset.} Per-trial additive constant simulates baseline drift. New augmentation: \texttt{x += N(0, offset\_std\textsuperscript{2})}. Not in current \texttt{augmentation.py} but trivial to add.
\item \textbf{Add Gaussian smoothing.} Temporal smoothing with width=2.0 reduces high-frequency noise while preserving speech-relevant dynamics ($<$100\,Hz).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{SSL training recipe}

\textbf{Pretraining data.} 456\,min raw continuous EDF across 29 patients (13 PS + 17 Lexical, zero patient overlap). Requires HGA extraction first (CAR $\to$ 70--150\,Hz filterbank $\to$ Hilbert $\to$ 200\,Hz $\to$ z-score). \textcolor{BrickRed}{Blocked on HGA extraction pipeline for raw EDF (not yet implemented).}

\textbf{Training.} AdamW, lr=$5 \times 10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-5}$, batch 64, cosine decay. Use bfloat16 mixed precision if available. Checkpoint selection: best validation $R^2$ on reconstruction.

\textbf{Transfer.} After SSL, discard the SSL reconstruction head. Transfer: shared Conv1d, per-patient $W^{(i)}/b^{(i)}$, Fourier PE, spatial/temporal blocks, $\Delta/\omega/e$. Initialize the supervised reconstruction decoder (Section~\ref{sec:recon-decoder}) and phoneme heads from scratch. Fine-tune with Phase 1 (source training) then Phase 2 (target adaptation) as before.

\subsection{Insights from seegnificant~\cite{mentzelopoulos2024}}\label{sec:seegnificant}

Mentzelopoulos et al.\ (NeurIPS 2024) solve the closest problem to ours in the literature: multi-subject neural decoding with variable electrode counts and positions, using MNI-based positional encoding and transformers. Key differences in regime:

\begin{table}[ht!]\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
& \textbf{seegnificant (sEEG)} & \textbf{Ours ($\mu$ECoG)} \\
\midrule
Modality & Depth electrodes (sEEG) & Surface grid ($\mu$ECoG) \\
Topology & Sparse, scattered 3D & Dense 2D grid, 1.3--1.7\,mm pitch \\
Electrodes/subject & 3--28 & 63--201 \\
Subjects & 21 (29 sessions) & 10--11 \\
Trials & 3{,}600+ & ${\sim}$1{,}300 \\
Task & Reaction time (regression) & 9-phoneme classification \\
Spatial scale & Whole-brain distributed & Focal vSMC (${\sim}$20$\times$40\,mm) \\
Params & 797K (753K shared) & ${\sim}$112K \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Their architecture:} per-electrode 1D Conv tokenizer ($K{=}2$), then alternating temporal self-attention and spatial self-attention ($\times L$ blocks), with RBF PE on MNI coordinates added before spatial attention. Per-subject regression heads (2{,}081 params each). No virtual electrodes, no reconstruction loss.

Three findings directly inform our ablation plan:

\textbf{(A) Spatial self-attention among real electrodes is their \#2 component ($\Delta R^2 = -0.10$ when ablated).} They use spatial \textit{self-attention} among all electrodes; we use \textit{cross-attention} from virtual electrodes to real electrodes. The approaches are complementary:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textit{Self-attention} (theirs): electrodes attend to each other directly. More flexible. But does not produce a fixed-size output --- they compress via MLP.
\item \textit{Cross-attention to VEs} (ours): forces information through 10 fixed anatomical bottlenecks. Fixed-size $L \times d$ output by construction. More constrained, more interpretable.
\end{itemize}
At our electrode counts (63--201), self-attention is still trivial: $201^2 \approx 40$K entries. \textbf{A14} tests their architecture on our data: per-electrode tokens + Fourier PE + spatial self-attention + MLP compression. No virtual electrodes. If A14 $\geq$ A3, VEs add complexity without benefit and should be dropped.

\textbf{(B) Per-subject heads are critical ($\Delta R^2 = -0.18$ when ablated).} Their per-subject regression head is 2{,}081 params each. Our v12 default now includes a per-patient input projection (${\sim}$4.2K/pt), closing the gap with the literature (seegnificant: 2K/subject, Willett: 65.8K/day, Levin: 262K/session). Three ablations diagnose where per-patient capacity is needed:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{A15} (remove input projection): ablate to 70 params/pt (6 geometric + 64 decoder embedding). Tests whether coordinates alone suffice without per-patient signal normalization. If A15 $\approx$ default, the projection is unnecessary.
\item \textbf{A16} (add output head): per-patient Linear($d \to 9$) classification head (${\sim}$600/pt) on top of the default input projection. Tests whether the shared backbone produces truly patient-agnostic representations, or whether the neural-to-phoneme mapping varies per patient.
\item \textbf{A17} (both): default input projection + A16 output head (${\sim}$4.8K/pt total). Maximum per-patient capacity. Risk: $N{=}10$ patients may allow memorization.
\end{itemize}
If A15 drops substantially, the projection is load-bearing (variability is in measurement). If A16 helps further, the ``phoneme features are universal'' assumption is wrong.

\textbf{(C) PE provides only $\Delta R^2 = -0.02$ (p\,=\,0.73, not significant).} This is a calibration warning: in their 21-subject, 3{,}600-trial setup, MNI positional encoding barely helps. However, their task (reaction time from widely distributed sEEG) is different from ours (phoneme classification from focal somatotopic cortex). Position should matter \textit{more} for somatotopic discrimination (tongue vs lip M1 are ${\sim}$5\,mm apart) than for distributed reaction time networks. Additionally, their spatial self-attention can implicitly discover spatial relationships from signal correlations even without PE. Our A3 vs A2 ablation will settle this empirically.

\textbf{What this does \textit{not} change:} Fourier PE is validated as equivalent to RBF PE (both $R^2 = 0.39$). Factored time/space attention beats joint 2D ($\Delta R^2 = +0.06$, $5.5\times$ faster). Per-electrode Conv1d tokenization is validated. The shared-backbone + per-subject-adaptation paradigm is validated.

\textcolor{BrickRed}{\textit{Topology caveat:}} sEEG electrodes are sparse points scattered in 3D (no local grid structure to exploit). $\mu$ECoG electrodes are a dense 2D grid with known precise local topology (1.3--1.7\,mm pitch). Their architecture deliberately avoids 2D conv because sEEG has no grid. For us, the grid is real and Conv2d exploits it --- the question is whether per-electrode tokenization with MNI PE (discarding the grid) outperforms Conv2d (exploiting the grid). This is the A1 vs A2/A3 comparison.

\section{Priorities and Risks}\label{sec:priorities}

\subsection{Priority ordering}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Coordinate architecture (A1--A6).} The central bet. Highest novelty.

\item \textbf{SSL pretraining (A18--A20).} Unlocks 456\,min of continuous data. \textcolor{BrickRed}{Blocked on HGA extraction.} Once unblocked, BIT-style temporal masking is the proven approach. Lower risk than architecture because the SSL backbone \textit{is} the v12 architecture.

\item \textbf{Per-patient capacity (A15--A17).} Default now includes input projection (${\sim}$4.2K/pt), matching literature consensus. A15 ablates down to test if coordinates alone suffice. Low risk, high diagnostic value.

\item \textbf{Cross-task data pooling (A13).} Same phonemes, overlapping patients~\cite{duraivel2025}. Directly addresses data scarcity. Works with any architecture.

\item \textbf{Population-level evaluation (A7).} Required to know if we have a real effect.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Hard gate}

All MNI-dependent claims require extracting and auditing electrode coordinates from BioImage Suite (DBS) / Brainlab (craniotomy). If coordinates are unavailable or too noisy, the fallback is A2 (Perceiver on scaled grid positions with no MNI).

\subsection{Success criteria}

\textbf{Primary:} Population mean PER improves $\geq 3\pp$ over Conv2d+BiGRU baseline, $\geq 3$ seeds, paired Wilcoxon $\alpha{=}0.05$.

\textbf{Mechanistic:} A3 $>$ A2 (coordinates help). A4 $>$ A3 (reconstruction helps). Learned $\Delta^{(i)}$ magnitudes consistent with known registration error ($< 15$\,mm).
